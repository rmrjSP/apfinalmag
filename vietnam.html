Vietnamese War

The Vietnam War lasted a horrific 21 years from 1954 to 1975. Vietnam was under the colonial rule of the French since the 19th century. In World War II the Japanese invaded Vietnam. A political leader named Ho Chi Minh founded a communist group, the Viet Minh, also called the League for the Independence of Vietnam. It was formed to fight off the Japanese and the French colonial rule in Vietnam. After World War II Japan withdrew from Vietnam. Ho Chi Minhâ€™s communist party, the Viet Minh, rose in power taking over the northern city of Hanoi, declaring a Democratic Republic of Vietnam, and setting Ho as the president. The prior emperor, Emperor Bao Dai, had France as back up to help set up the southern city of Saigon as the capital of Vietnam. Ho and Bao both tried to unify Vietnam but with different national models. Ho supported communism and Bao wanted a close economy and culture to the West. In July 1954 the Geneva Agreements were signed to divide Vietnam in two, Ho controlled the North and Bao controlled the South. In 1955 a strong anti-communist politician names Ngo Dinh Diem became the new president of South Vietnam. Dwight D. Eisenhower was the United States president at the time, as he was against any Soviet Union allies, he supported South Vietnam. Training and equipment were sent to South Vietnam from the American military to stop any Vietnamese Communist in the south. These Vietnamese Communist were called Viet Cong and were often tortured and executed. In late 1960 Diem had many people against him causing the formation of the National Liberation Front. This organization claimed not to be associated with the communist party but many in Washington assumed it was mostly communist. John F. Kennedy continued to support South Vietnam in fear of the domino theory. If Vietnam fell to communism so would all closely tied Southeast Asian country. A success in Vietnam was a last resort to prevent the spread of communism after the United States has gone through multiple defeats throughout the Cold War. The United States strongly supported South Vietnam, sending considerable number of weapons and forces. Of course, throughout the war North Vietnam had its supporters. The Soviet Union would send over resources and supplies over to North Vietnam. China sent over troops that would build defense infrastructure in North Vietnam. By June 1965, 82,000 American troops were stationed in Vietnam. Growing anti-war movements began to rise in the United States but there were still thousands of troops of being sent over to fight in Vietnam. Over 250,000 Americans peacefully protested in Washington, D.C. for the withdrawal of American troops in Vietnam. Protests grew across the United States against the war in Vietnam, but that did not stop Nixon from authorizing bombing raids against Hanoi and Haiphong in Vietnam. It was not until 1972 that Nixon ended draft calls to the war. January 27th, 1973, the Agreement on Ending War and Restoring Peace in Vietnam had been signed by United States representatives as well as representatives of the South Vietnamese communist forces, North Vietnam, and South Vietnam. All U.S. forces were withdrawn from North Vietnam and South Vietnam within sixty days. Nixon resigned presidency in 1974 after an embarrassing loss in the Vietnam war. On July 1975 North and South Vietnam were unified under communist rule as the Socialist Republic of Vietnam.
